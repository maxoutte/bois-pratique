Straight cuts are often talked about as if they come down to natural hand skill. In real shop work, they usually come down to something far less dramatic: a clear line, stable support, a believable guide, a decent blade and a body position that does not fall apart halfway through the cut.

When a cut goes bad, one of those pieces is usually the real cause.

## Marking is not a formality

Layout gets rushed because it feels quiet and unglamorous. But a good cut starts there.

A useful cut line should be:

- clear
- thin
- tied to the correct waste side
- consistent with how the cut will actually be guided

A vague line forces vague cutting.

## Support matters before the saw ever starts

If the workpiece shifts, sags or pinches near the end of the cut, the result gets worse fast. Before turning on the tool, it helps to know:

- what is supported
- what will fall away
- what must stay clean
- what might pinch the blade

A lot of bad cuts are really bad setups.

## Guidance beats confidence

Beginners often try to cut straight by concentration alone. That rarely works as well as they hope.

A proper guide, rail or clamped straightedge removes hesitation and reduces mid-cut correction. That usually improves:

- consistency
- comfort
- cut quality
- confidence

A good guide does more than a determined wrist.

## A blade tells the truth quickly

When a cut burns, drifts or tears out badly, people often blame the saw or their technique first. Very often the blade is already telling the story.

A dull or poorly chosen blade creates:

- more effort
- more tear-out
- more heat
- more deviation

That is not a side issue. It is part of the cutting system.

## My direct take

If you want straighter cuts, spend less time hoping your hands will compensate and more time improving the setup. Straight cutting is not a mystery skill. It is usually the result of a cleaner cutting environment.
