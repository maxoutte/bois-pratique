Sanding suffers from a strange problem: everyone assumes they understand it, which is exactly why it gets done badly so often. It is treated like the uninteresting step before the real finishing work, even though it is already part of the finish quality.

A badly sanded piece can look acceptable at first, then look disappointing the moment light catches the surface or finish reveals what was left behind.

## Bad sanding usually hides well at first

That is what makes it deceptive. A surface can look fine and still carry:

- scratches from the previous grit
- uneven low spots
- rounded edges that were meant to stay crisp
- overloaded abrasives
- dust that keeps scratching instead of clearing away

By the time those issues show clearly, the piece often feels “almost done,” which makes correction more annoying.

## Sanding is about removing, not insisting

The job is not to rub harder. It is to remove the evidence of the previous step without damaging the geometry of the workpiece.

For many ordinary projects, a simple grit progression is enough:

- 80 for rough or heavily marked surfaces
- 120 to clean things up seriously
- 180 for a sound pre-finish surface
- 220 when there is a specific reason to go there

Skipping too far between grits is one of the most common causes of disappointing results.

## Random-orbit sanders work best with restraint

Pushing harder rarely helps. It usually makes the result worse.

A better approach is:

- light pressure
- slow movement
- overlapping passes
- a flat pad that stays flat

The best sanding rarely looks dramatic.

## My direct take

Good sanding is not exciting. It is patient, readable and slightly disciplined. If your finish quality is inconsistent, the problem is often here long before it is in the finish itself.
